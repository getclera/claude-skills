# Interviewer Research (Public Work)

## What This Skill Does

Goes beyond a résumé to find what the interviewer has **said and written publicly** — conference
talks, podcast appearances, blog posts, published articles, interviews, notable social posts — and
synthesizes it into prep: their actual views, what they care about, the language they use, and how to
have a conversation that resonates.

This is the difference between knowing someone's job title and knowing how they *think*. A candidate
who can say "I listened to your talk on X and it changed how I think about Y" is unforgettable.

Pairs with `interviewer-dossier` (which reads their LinkedIn). This skill is the web-research half.

---

## Gather Context First

1. **Interviewer's full name** (required).
2. **Their company and role** (required) — to disambiguate from others with the same name. Common
   names need this badly.
3. **Anything to narrow the search** — their domain/specialty, a personal site or handle if known.
4. **The role being interviewed for** — so the synthesis is aimed at what matters.

---

## Process

1. **Search deliberately and verify identity.** Search the name + company, name + "podcast," name +
   "talk," name + "blog," name + their domain. **Confirm you've got the right person** before
   attributing anything — same-name mixups are the main failure mode. If you can't confirm identity,
   say so rather than guessing.
2. **Pull their actual content** — fetch the talks, posts, and interviews you find. Read for: their
   stated views, recurring themes, what they're excited or critical about, and how they talk.
3. **Synthesize, don't transcribe.** Paraphrase their views in your own words (respect copyright —
   short attributed quotes only, sparingly).
4. **Translate to prep** — what this tells the candidate about what to emphasize, and 2–3 genuine,
   specific references the candidate could make.

> **Privacy & accuracy:** Only public, professional content. No digging into personal/private life.
> Flag confidence — if a source might be a different person of the same name, say so. Never fabricate
> a talk, quote, or article.

---

## Output Format

```
# What [Name] Says Publicly
*(Public sources only. Identity confidence: [high/medium — note any ambiguity].)*

## Their Themes
[The 3–5 things they consistently care about, in your words, with where you found each.]

## Their Stated Views
[Specific positions they've taken — paraphrased. What they champion, what they push back on.]

## How They Talk
[Tone, vocabulary, what excites them — so the candidate can match register.]

## Genuine References You Could Make
[2–3 specific, true things the candidate could reference — "your point on X in [source]" — that show
real engagement without being sycophantic.]

## Sources
[List with links, so the candidate can go read/listen themselves before the interview.]
```

---

## Quality Bar / Failure Checklist

This research FAILS if it:
- ❌ Attributes content to the wrong same-named person (verify identity first)
- ❌ Fabricates talks, quotes, or articles
- ❌ Reproduces large chunks of their writing instead of paraphrasing (copyright)
- ❌ Pulls in personal/private material rather than public professional work
- ❌ Produces generic themes not grounded in actual sources the candidate can check
- ❌ Suggests sycophantic references instead of genuine intellectual engagement
