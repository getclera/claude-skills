# Career Pivot Translator

## What This Skill Does

The hardest part of a career pivot isn't lacking transferable skills — it's **language.** A teacher
moving into product, a consultant into ops, a military officer into corporate, an academic into
industry: they often have exactly the right underlying capabilities but describe them in their old
field's vocabulary, so recruiters in the new field don't recognize the fit.

This skill does the translation: it finds the *genuine* transferable value and reframes it in the
target field's terms — specific resume bullets, a positioning narrative, and an honest read on what
genuinely transfers vs. what they'll need to build.

The bar is honesty. Translation, not inflation. A teacher who "managed a classroom" can credibly
claim stakeholder management and performance coaching — but not "5 years of B2B SaaS experience."

---

## Gather Maximum Context First

1. **Their full background** (required) — resume + the real texture of what they did day to day. The
   day-to-day detail matters more than titles; that's where transferable skills hide.
2. **The target field/role** (required) — ideally a specific JD, so the translation aims at real
   requirements.
3. **Why the pivot** — the motivation often reveals genuine transferable passion and a narrative hook.
4. **What they're worried about** — usually the gaps they think disqualify them (often they don't).

Push for specifics on the day-to-day. "I was a teacher" hides the transferable gold; "I ran a
classroom of 30, managed parent relationships, designed a year-long curriculum, and tracked
individual progress against goals" reveals it.

---

## Process

1. **Mine the real work**, not the titles. For each thing they actually did, ask: what underlying
   capability does this demonstrate? (Managing a classroom → stakeholder management, performance
   coaching, operating under pressure, communication.)
2. **Map to the target field's language.** Learn what the target role actually values, then translate
   each genuine capability into that vocabulary.
3. **Be honest about the line** between what transfers (reframe confidently) and what doesn't (they'll
   need to build or learn it — name it).
4. **Build the narrative.** A pivot needs a story that makes the move make sense — why this field, why
   now, why them. The "why" turns a random jump into a deliberate path.
5. **Produce concrete bullets** the candidate can put on a resume and say in an interview.

---

## Output Format

```
# Career Pivot: [Current Field] → [Target Field/Role]

## What Genuinely Transfers
For each: **[What you did]** → **[The capability]** → **[How it reads in {target field}]**
[Concrete, resume-ready bullets in the target field's language.]

## Your Pivot Narrative
[The story that makes this move make sense — why this field, why now, why you. 3–4 sentences they can
deliver in an interview.]

## What You'll Need to Build (the honest part)
[Genuine gaps the translation can't cover — skills/knowledge to learn, and how to start closing them
or address them in interviews.]

## Resume Bullets (target-field language)
[5–8 rewritten bullets that translate real experience into terms the new field recognizes.]
```

---

## Quality Bar / Failure Checklist

This FAILS if it:
- ❌ Inflates experience into claims the candidate can't back up ("5 years SaaS" from zero)
- ❌ Stays in the old field's language instead of translating to the target field's
- ❌ Works from titles instead of mining the real day-to-day work
- ❌ Skips the honest "what you'll need to build" section
- ❌ Offers no coherent pivot narrative, leaving the move looking random
- ❌ Produces vague bullets instead of concrete, target-field-specific ones
