# Offer Decision Tool

## What This Skill Does

Helps someone make a high-stakes career decision — between offers, or offer vs. staying put — by going
**well beyond comp.** It weighs growth trajectory, manager quality, equity reality, risk, life fit,
and the things that actually determine whether a job is good, then produces a decision framed around
*this person's* priorities.

The bar is honest and rigorous: it models equity realistically (most early equity is worth little),
names risks plainly, and resists the pull to just pick the biggest number.

---

## Gather EVERYTHING First

A decision is only as good as the inputs. For **each** option (including "stay where I am"), collect:

1. **Comp** — base, bonus, sign-on, and the full equity details: number of shares/options, strike
   price, current/last valuation, vesting schedule, cliff.
2. **The role** — title, scope, what they'd actually do, growth path, who they'd report to.
3. **The company** — stage, funding, runway if known, trajectory, market.
4. **The manager** — what they know about the person they'd report to. (This often matters more than
   comp and people under-weight it.)
5. **Life fit** — location, remote/onsite, hours, commute, travel, team.
6. **Risk** — job security, company stability, how reversible the decision is.
7. **The person's priorities** — what they actually care about right now (money? growth? stability?
   learning? a specific manager? lifestyle?). This is the weighting that drives everything.

Push for the equity specifics especially — people gloss over them, and they're where offers most
differ in real value. If they don't know their equity details, tell them exactly what to ask for.

---

## Process

1. **Get the priorities first** — the weighting matters more than the data. What does this person
   actually want from their next move?
2. **Lay out each option** across all dimensions, side by side.
3. **Model the equity honestly.** Don't hand-wave "equity upside." Walk through what it could be worth
   at different outcomes, and stress that most startup equity ends up worth little — so weight it as
   risk-adjusted, not face value. (This is illustrative modeling, not financial advice — say so.)
4. **Weight against their priorities**, not generic "what's best." A 20% raise might lose to a worse-
   paid role under a great manager if growth is what they want.
5. **Give a clear recommendation with the reasoning** — not a wishy-washy "it depends." Take a
   position, show the logic, and name what would change the answer.

> **Boundary:** This is decision-framing, not financial advice. Equity modeling is illustrative. For
> the actual financial/tax implications of equity, suggest they consult a professional.

## Output Format

```
# Offer Decision: [Option A] vs [Option B] vs [Stay]

## Your Priorities (the weighting)
[What this person actually cares about, ranked — this drives the whole analysis.]

## Side by Side
| Dimension | Option A | Option B | Stay |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total cash | | | |
| Equity (risk-adjusted) | | | |
| Role / growth | | | |
| Manager | | | |
| Company risk | | | |
| Life fit | | | |

## The Equity, Honestly
[Realistic modeling per option. What it could be worth, weighted by likelihood. The plain truth that
most early equity is worth $0 — and which of these offers' equity is more/less likely to pay off.]

## The Call
[A clear recommendation, the reasoning tied to their priorities, and what would flip the decision.]

## What to Verify Before You Sign
[Any missing info — especially equity details — they should nail down first.]
```

---

## Quality Bar / Failure Checklist

This FAILS if it:
- ❌ Just picks the highest-comp option without weighing growth, manager, risk, and fit
- ❌ Hand-waves equity as "upside" instead of modeling it risk-adjusted and honestly
- ❌ Weights against generic "best" instead of the person's actual priorities
- ❌ Gives a wishy-washy "it depends" instead of a clear recommendation with reasoning
- ❌ Skips collecting the full details — especially equity specifics — before advising
- ❌ Presents equity modeling as financial advice instead of flagging it as illustrative
