# Rejection Email Writer

## What This Skill Does

Writes **candidate rejections that don't damage your reputation.** Most rejections are cold,
generic, and remembered for the wrong reasons — and rejected candidates talk, refer, reapply, and
sometimes become customers. A good rejection is warm, appropriately specific, and leaves the person
feeling respected. This is an employer-brand play disguised as an admin task.

---

## Process

1. **Get the stage:** how far did the candidate get? Applied / screened / interviewed / final round.
   Tone and specificity scale with stage — a final-round candidate deserves real feedback; an
   applicant gets a warm, brief no.
2. **Get the reason** (for the user's reference and to calibrate specificity). Some reasons can be
   shared kindly; some shouldn't be detailed (legal/judgment calls).
3. **Draft to match the stage.** Warmer and more specific the further they got.
4. **Decide on feedback.** Offer concrete, kind feedback for late-stage candidates *if* the user
   wants to give it. Never give feedback that creates legal exposure or reads as a discrimination
   rationale.

> **Boundary:** Drafts only — the user sends. Don't promise feedback the user hasn't agreed to give.

---

## How to Write a Good Rejection

- **Scale warmth and specificity to stage.** A final-round candidate who spent hours deserves more
  than a form letter. An early applicant gets brief and kind.
- **Be human, not corporate.** "We went with someone whose experience lined up more closely with
  what this specific role needs right now" beats "we have decided to move forward with other
  candidates."
- **Specific praise where it's true.** Naming something you genuinely valued softens the no and
  rings true.
- **Leave the door open if you mean it** — "I'd love to stay in touch for future roles" — but only if
  it's real. A false door is worse than a clean close.
- **Keep feedback safe.** Concrete and forward-looking ("the bar on X was very high this round"), never
  anything that could read as a protected-characteristic rationale. When in doubt, keep it brief.
- **Never ghost.** The whole point is that closing the loop *well* is the brand win.

---

## Output Format

```
# Rejection: [Candidate] — [Stage]
[subject line]

[Warm opening — thank them genuinely, by name.]

[The no, stated kindly and clearly — no false hope, no burying it.]

[Stage-appropriate specifics: praise where true; feedback if the user wants to give it.]

[Door open, if genuine.]

[Warm close.]

*Draft only — review and send yourself. Feedback included only if you've chosen to give it.*
```

---

## Rejection Failure Checklist

A rejection FAILS if it:
- ❌ Reads as a generic form letter to a candidate who got far enough to deserve better
- ❌ Uses cold corporate phrasing ("move forward with other candidates")
- ❌ Buries or softens the no into false hope
- ❌ Gives feedback that could read as a protected-characteristic rationale
- ❌ Opens a door the user doesn't actually mean
- ❌ Promises feedback the user hasn't agreed to give
- ❌ Gets sent automatically instead of handed to the user
