# Job Description Writer

## The Core Principle

A great JD is a **sales document, not a checklist.** Strong candidates have options. The JD's job is
to make them *want* this one. That means: lead with momentum and mission, make the role feel
consequential, and be specific enough that the right person self-identifies and the wrong person
self-selects out.

Order matters: **why the company is winning → the vision → the team → the product → the role → what
they'll actually do → qualifications → perks → culture.** Qualifications come late on purpose. By the
time a great candidate reaches them, they're already sold and reading to confirm fit.

---

## The Template (Use This Structure)

ALWAYS follow this section order. Adapt section depth to seniority and company stage, but keep the
sequence.

```
# [Role Title]
**Location · Employment type · Location type · Department**

## tl;dr: Why choose [Company]?
[2–3 punchy bullets with PROOF of momentum: funding + notable backers, launch traction,
recognizable customers, press. Use real numbers and real names. This is the hook.]

## About [Company]
[2–4 sentences. What the company does, in plain language. What problem it removes and for whom.
Name the backers if notable.]

## The vision
[The bigger picture. A vivid analogy or a founder quote works well here. Make the reader feel the
size of the opportunity.]

## The team
[Who they'll work with and why that's exciting. Pedigree, density of talent, how the team operates.
A quote from an investor or founder lands well. Where you work.]

## The product
[Concrete outcomes customers have achieved. Specific numbers beat adjectives.]

## The Role: [Title]
[Why this role exists NOW and why it matters. Who they report to, who they work with. Frame them as
a strategic partner, not a task-doer. Paint the picture of what great looks like.]

## Key Responsibilities
[5–8 bullets. Each starts with a strong verb. Each describes ownership and outcome, not activity.
"Build systems for X" beats "Responsible for X."]

## Basic Qualifications
[The genuine must-haves. Keep this list honest and short — every extra requirement filters out a
strong candidate who'd have thrived. Write them as the person, not the resume:
"You write well" beats "Strong written communication skills."]

## Preferred Qualifications
[Nice-to-haves. The "you'll really thrive if..." list.]

## [Company] in the press
[Links / credibility markers, if any.]

## What we offer
[Comp philosophy, equity, PTO, benefits, the human stuff. Be specific and real.]

## Our Culture
[Who the team is as people. This is where you show personality. Specific > generic.]
```

---

## Writing Rules

1. **Lead with proof, not promises.** "Backed by Accel, the partner who led Scale, Vercel, and
   Sentry" beats "well-funded startup." Names and numbers build trust instantly.
2. **Write qualifications as the person, not the résumé.** "You can read our product — when someone
   says 'the editor is weird,' you can figure out what they mean" beats "product intuition required."
   This is the single biggest differentiator of a great JD.
3. **Keep must-haves honest and short.** Every requirement you add filters out someone strong. If
   it's not truly required, move it to preferred or cut it.
4. **Make the role feel consequential.** Say why it exists *now* and what it determines. "The
   customers signing up now will determine whether our reputation spreads" makes a CS role feel like
   it matters.
5. **Use specific numbers everywhere.** Pipeline generated, customers named, conversion lifts, team
   size, funding. Specificity signals a company that's actually winning.
6. **Show personality in culture.** Generic "we work hard and play hard" is worse than nothing. Real
   detail — "after work we're avid readers, photographers, acapella singers, film critics who don't
   hold back" — makes people picture themselves there.
7. **Frame the candidate as a partner.** The best JDs describe a trusted advisor / strategic partner,
   not a ticket-closer. "The ideal scenario is when customers call you before making any major
   decision" sells autonomy and trust.

## Market Benchmarking (Optional — use TheirStack MCP)

If the user wants the JD grounded in what competitors are actually offering, use the **TheirStack
MCP** to pull live job-posting data:
- What similar roles at comparable companies require and pay
- Which skills/tools competitors list for the same role
- How the company's offer stacks up

If TheirStack isn't connected, say so and suggest enabling it, then write the JD from the brief alone.
Don't invent benchmark numbers.

---

## Process

1. **Gather the brief.** Role, seniority, location, reporting line, what success looks like, the
   company's traction/backers/customers. Ask for what's missing — especially the proof points, since
   they power the tl;dr.
2. **Optionally benchmark** via TheirStack.
3. **Draft using the template**, applying every writing rule.
4. **Run the failure checklist.**
5. **Deliver as a clean, paste-ready JD.**

---

## JD Failure Checklist

A JD FAILS if it:
- ❌ Leads with a wall of requirements instead of why the company is winning
- ❌ Lists must-haves that aren't truly required (filtering out strong candidates)
- ❌ Writes qualifications as résumé keywords ("strong communicator") instead of as the person
- ❌ Uses generic adjectives ("fast-paced," "dynamic") instead of specific proof
- ❌ Makes the role sound like task execution instead of ownership
- ❌ Has a culture section that could belong to any company
- ❌ Buries or omits the mission/vision that makes a great candidate care
